ENGR 482 — Soft Robotics

Soft Robotic Bottle-Cap Gripper & Twister

Academic Project

Designed a hydraulically actuated soft robotic system to grip and twist a 28mm plastic bottle cap using only soft materials — no rigid motors or gears. The system consists of two independently actuated components: an inflatable ring gripper and a fibre-reinforced twisting actuator, both fabricated from Ecoflex 00-30 silicone.

  • Evaluated three design concepts via weighted decision matrix (grip capability, torque generation, soft compliance, modelling feasibility, reliability) — selected Concept 2 (inflatable ring + fibre-reinforced twister) scoring 4.15/5 for its uniform grip and predictable torque.
  • Analytically modelled grip force (F = P × A) estimating 75–150N at 30–60 kPa, and torque (τ = r·F·μ) exceeding the 0.3–0.6 Nm requirement; modelled twisting angle as θ = k·P showing linear pressure-rotation response.
  • Designed fabrication process: two-part 3D-printed silicone moulds for the ring gripper, nylon thread helically wrapped at 35° fibre angle on cylindrical actuator to constrain radial expansion and induce torsional deformation under pressure.
  • Specified materials (Ecoflex 00-30), assembly, watertight sealing requirements, and an independent dual-pressure routing system allowing grip and twist to be controlled separately to prevent slip before rotation.
  • Developed comprehensive test plan covering grip force (load sensor), torque output (lever arm + force gauge), angular displacement (visual markers), and repeatability over 10–20 cycles.

ENGR 406 — Introduction to MEMS

Stacked Chevron Actuator — MEMS Displacement Study

Academic / Research

Investigated whether stacking multiple tiers of chevron actuators in a MEMS device produces proportionally greater displacement than a single-layer standard chevron actuator — fabricated using UBC's in-house 1.5-layer SU-8 photolithography process and physically tested in the lab.

  • Designed the stacked chevron actuator layout using the in-house 1.5-layer SU-8 process, considering geometric constraints, beam angles, anchor placement, and the thermal expansion mechanism that drives displacement.
  • Performed structural and thermal analysis — calculating expected displacement as a function of stacking number (n), chevron beam angle, and applied current — to predict whether linear or superlinear displacement scaling would occur.
  • Fabricated the device through the photolithography process in a cleanroom environment, then tested the physical chip to measure actual displacement versus theoretical predictions.
  • Compared single-stack vs. multi-stack output displacement, documenting failure modes, fabrication deviations, and the relationship between design parameters and measured performance.
  • Produced a final documentation package including literature search, design layouts, analysis, test plan with labelled test points, results, and lessons learned for future MEMS designers.

Biomedical ENGR 482 Research
ENGR 482 — Engineering Design

Transcatheter Heart Valve — Engineering Analysis Report

Research Report

Authored a comprehensive engineering report on transcatheter heart valve (THV) design, covering the biomechanics of the native aortic valve, engineering design requirements for TAVR/TMVR systems, device architecture and materials, deployment mechanics, failure modes, and future directions — integrating fluid dynamics, materials science, and structural mechanics.

  • Modelled native aortic valve biomechanics: calculated membrane stress (σ = Pr/t ≈ 400 kPa at 120 mmHg) across 35M cycles/year, quantified pressure gradients using ΔP ≈ 4v² and turbulence thresholds in stenotic conditions (v > 3 m/s).
  • Analysed frame material trade-offs (Nitinol vs. Cobalt-Chromium): shape memory vs. balloon-expandable deployment, radial force magnitude, flexibility, and anatomical conformity — relating material selection to clinical outcomes.
  • Compared TAVR and TMVR engineering challenges — TAVR's tubular-root compatibility vs. TMVR's 3D saddle annulus geometry, LVOT obstruction risk, and static vs. dynamic radial force requirements.
  • Identified root causes of clinical complications: poor personalized fit, low delivery manoeuvrability, and absence of real-time repositioning — proposing patient-specific 3D-printed geometries, bioresorbable frames, and adaptive delivery systems as solutions.
  • Synthesised engineering design principles applicable across all transcatheter valve systems: anatomical compatibility over standardised sizing, hemodynamic mimicry of native valve behaviour, and material fatigue resistance as primary design drivers.
